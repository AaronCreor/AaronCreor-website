Measuring AI in My LinkedIn Feed with Python

Published Mar 7, 2026

All Posts Home

Post

I kept noticing a pattern in my LinkedIn feed: a lot of posts felt polished in the same way. Similar hooks, similar pacing, similar calls to action, and a growing amount of obvious AI-adjacent language. Instead of guessing, I decided to measure it.

The result was LinkedInAIAnalysis, a small Python utility that logs into LinkedIn, samples posts from the feed, scores them for likely AI-assisted writing patterns, and generates charts from the results.

How It Works

The script uses Playwright to open a logged-in LinkedIn session, scroll the feed, and collect post content. From there, it applies a deliberately aggressive heuristic scorer. The goal is not to prove authorship or detect AI with certainty, but to estimate how much of the feed looks shaped by AI-driven writing patterns.

The heuristic looks for signals like explicit AI keywords, list-heavy formatting, templated CTA language, repetitive phrasing, high line-break density, and the kinds of punchy hooks that have become common in AI-assisted social posts.

What I Found

On my own sample, a meaningful share of posts landed in the medium and high AI-likelihood buckets. What stood out most was that the strongest signals were often stylistic rather than topical. Some posts never mentioned ChatGPT, Claude, or AI at all, but still looked heavily optimized around the same structure and cadence.

That was the interesting part for me. The feed is not just filling with posts about AI. It is increasingly filled with posts that feel written in a converging style, whether they were fully generated, lightly assisted, or just modeled after the same successful AI-era templates.

Why I Built It

I like small tools that answer questions I am already asking myself. This project was a good fit because it combined browser automation, lightweight text analysis, and data visualization in a way that produced something immediately interpretable.

It also turned into a nice example of the practical engineering work that shows up in small projects: handling login state, dealing with dynamic scroll containers, adjusting brittle selectors, and shaping the final output into something clean enough to share publicly.
